Report: The impact of weather and bicycle traffic on bicycle theft in Berlin

Introduction

Motivation

Bicycle theft is a huge problem in Berlin, as it has one of the highest rates of bike theft per inhabitant in Germany. This poses the question: How can one keep their bicycle safe in Berlin? Apart from a strong lock and a safe parking spot, there might be other influences on the probability of a bike being stolen, such as place or time of the day. A rather uncommon factor to think of is the weather, though it plays a huge part in mobility and transportation, especially for bicycles. Hypotheses that come to mind are:

  • Less bikes are stolen on a rainy or snowy day.
  • More bikes are stolen on sunny days as more people will ride their bike
  • Thieves might be more reluctant to go out in the rain as well, and therefore, it might be safer for ones bicycle in the rain

To answer questions derived from these hypotheses, this project will combine three datasets from Berlin from 2022: one containing bicycle theft data, one containing different weather metrics and one containing bicycle traffic counts, look for anomalies and correlations in the data and try to draw conclusions from the results.

Research questions

This report (mostly) follows the IMRAD structure, with subsections under Results and Discussion dedicated to answering the following research questions:

  • RC1: Does more bicycle traffic lead to more bicycle thefts?
  • RC2: Are there times where bikes, relative to traffic, are stolen more often?
  • RC3: Do higher temperatures lead to more bicycle traffic?
  • RC4: Do higher temperatures increase the risk of bicycle theft?
  • RC5: Does more rain lead to less or more bicycle traffic?
  • RC6: Does more rain increase the risk of bicycle theft?
  • RC7: Are there significant differences in weather metrics at times when bikes were stolen?

Methods

The datasets

All data sets used are available under Open Data licenses that require attribution.

Weather data

License: Nutzungsbedingungen fr Daten im CDC-OpenData Bereich

The weather data is from Deutscher Wetterdienst, a german goverment institution that is collecting and sharing weather data. They provide, amongst other data, hourly measurements of the temperature, humidity and percipitation, along with the type of percipitation (snow, rain) for different weather stations. The data for percipitation is provided in a seperate file from the air temperature data.
As this analysis is focused on Berlin, the data used is from the station at Alexanderplatz in Berlin, as it is fairly central point in Berlin. Since the publication of bicycle theft data only started in the end of 2021, only weather data from the timespan January 1st 2022 to December 31 2022 considered in this analysis.

Bicycle theft data

License: CC BY 3.0 DE DEED

The city of Berlin provides a large amount of Open Data, and one of the most popular datasets is the bicycle theft dataset used in this project. To my knowledge, Berlin is also the only city in Germany that provides per-incident data on bicycle theft, most other cities only publish aggregated data which limits the space for analysis.
The dataset is updated daily and provides extensive information about each datapoint, namely:

  • the timeframe in which the theft occured (i.e. the time when the bike was last seen and when it was first discovered stolen), hourly
  • the LOR code (a identifier) of the part of the city in which the theft occured
  • the damage caused by the theft in euros
  • the type of bike stolen
  • if the theft was only an attempted theft
  • the type of theft, f.e. if the theft involved breaking into private property

Bicycle count data

License: Datenlizenz Deutschland Namensnennung Version 2.0

The city of Berlin also provides hourly data from bicycle counting stations across the city. Data from a station near Alexanderplatz (namely, Karl-Marx-Allee) is used to analyze the effects of the mobility on bicycle theft f.e. if the number of bicycle riders is correlated with the number of bikes stolen. This station is also one of the most popular station in terms of bicycles counted per day.
As of January the 7th 2023, the dataset only contains bicycle counts up to the year 2022.

For this project, an automated data pipeline which is contained in the above mentioned project/pipeline.py. This pipeline will download the datasets (or use a already downloaded version if present), apply preprocessing steps and export the result to the respective table in the SQLite database contained in data/bt_and_weather.sqlite.   • Extract the required files from the downloaded ZIP archives
  • Create the union of equal datasets to allow for possible extension to multiple weather stations etc.

  • Drop unnecessary columns measuring formal quality and convert binary column to boolean
  • For the type of percipitation: map the numbers to their meaning (taken from dataset specification), f.e. 7 == "solid" (meaning snow)
  • Translate the remaining column names

  • Timestamps have seperate columns for date and hour -> join into a single column for timestamp
  • Translate the values in the "VERSUCH" column to english
  • Translate remaining column names to english
  • Drop old unnecessary columns
  • Check if the dataset is too new:
    • The dataset is updated daily by appending new data
    • At some point in the future, the maintainers could decide to archive old data seperately and the downloaded data could then be missing information from 2022

Analysing the data

Assumptions

To simpify the analysis, a few assumptions have to be made.

First, it is assumed that the weather at Alexanderplatz is representative for Berlin as a whole. This is necessary as the other option would be using all the weather stations in Berlin, as well as using another dataset for translating the LOR location codes into coordinates. For the scope of this project, this seems excessive and would likely result in minimal differences.

Second, the same assumption is make about bicycle traffic, meaning that bicycle traffic at Karl-Marx-Allee is representative of Berlin as whole. When put in relation to the weather, this assumption should be fairly reliable as the station is quite close to the weather station used and is likely to serve as a good indicator for this relationship.

Challenges

The bicycle theft data contains time data in the the form of timeframes. For example, many thefts occur overnight, which means timeframe could be from the evening to the next morning. The weather data and the bicycle count data however is hourly, which means that it needs to be aggregated somehow for it to combined with the other data. As the length of the timeframes varies, the mean was chosen for aggregation (see results for RC7). In most of the analysis, the aggregation is done by grouping per calendar week an then aggregating this. For the bicycle theft data, the time at the start of the timeframe is used for grouping.

Analysis

For suitable relations, the correlation coefficent is calculated. Even though this deviates from the IMRAD structure, the descriptions of the individual analysis are provided along with the results for better readability.

RC1: Does more bicycle traffic lead to more bicycle thefts?

The mean daily bicycle theft and counts are aggregated per calendar week and plotted. They are intentionally not aggregated per week directly as the first and last week of the year might be shorter than the other weeks. Additionaly, the correlation is calculated.

# First, calculate the size/sum per day and then take the mean of that
# This way, the first and last week of the year (which could have less than 7 days) are represented correctly
thefts_per_week = bt_2022.set_index("theft_begin_time").resample("D").size().resample("W").mean()

}, index=thefts_per_week.index)
correlation_bc_bt.index.name = "Week"
print("Correlation: {}".format(thefts_per_week.corr(bicycles_counted_per_week)))
correlation_bc_bt.plot()

RC2: Are there times where bikes, relative to traffic, are stolen more often?

From the mean daily thefts per week and mean daily bicycle count per week, a coefficent can be calculated which will be called the Bicycle Danger Index and expresses the number of thefts relative to the number of bike riders. This coeffient should express the relative risk of a bicycle being stolen and can be plotted to find seasonalities where bike theft is relatively speaking more likely. A Bike Danger Index higher than usual means that compared to the amount of bikes counted that week, more bikes than usual were stolen.

Discussion

  • RC1: Does more bicycle traffic lead to more bicycle thefts?
    • As expected, the result show that bicycle traffic is highly correlated with bicycle theft. This could mean that most bikes are stolen while they are parked somewhere "on the move", as opposed to being in a bicycle shed or similar, as in the latter case, the traffic should have less of an influence.
  • RC2: Are there times where bikes, relative to traffic, are stolen more often?
    • The plot shows a clear picture: In the colder months, where less people are riding their bikes, the risk of a bike being stolen is far higher.
    • One explaination could be that during the summer, the selection of bikes for bicycle thieves is higher and therefore, they might be more selective in which bikes they steal, instead of simply stealing more bikes.
    • By far the highest risk is observed close to Christmas and New Years Eve, where most people will either be staying with their family or for the latter, be drunk and (hopefully) not ride their bicycle. This does not seem to apply as much to bicycle thieves.
  • RC3: Do higher temperatures lead to more bicycle traffic?
    • Bicycle traffic also has a strong correlation with the air temperature, which is most likely explained by people prefering to ride their bike in favorable conditions. Interestingly, the summer with the highest temperature show a slight decrease, possible due to many days being too hot for enjoyable cycling.
  • RC4: Do higher temperatures increase the risk of bicycle theft?
    • This questions was indirectly answered in RC3, but when plotting the two graphs against each other, it can be seen that there is a slight inverse correlation between air temperature and thefts.
    • Especially when looking at some of the peaks in Bike Danger Index, some local minima can be observed for the temperature. This can be explained by the assumption that less people will ride their bike during colder times, but thefts will not decrease as much.
  • RC5: Does more rain lead to less or more bicycle traffic?
    • When looking at the peaks in rain amount per week, a similar observation to RC4 can be made. Unsuprisingly, large amounts of rain lead to less people riding their bike and vice versa, as most will likely prefer travelling by car or public transport instead.
    • However, this relationship is not as clear as the one between air temperature and bicycle traffic. A better is representation is the second graph measuring the hours of rain per week instead of the total amount of rain, where the decrease in bicycle riders can be observed at many different points.
  • RC6: Does more rain increase the risk of bicycle theft?
    • While this relationship also does not always hold true, there is a correlation between the amount of rainy hours and the risk of a bicycle being stolen. Again, many of the peaks correlate with an increased Bike Danger Index.
    • Once more, Bicycle thieves seem to be less reluctant of unfavorable weather then their victims, even during rainy weeks.
  • RC7: Are there significant differences in weather metrics at times when bikes were stolen?
    • In this analysis, the approach was aggregating based on individual theft cases' timeframes rather than aggregating by week. The comparison of weather metrics confirms the observations from the other research questions.
    • The comparison of bicycle counts is shows an interesting observation to be investiaged: during times where bicycles were stolen, the mean number of counted bicycles was higher than the overall mean for the year 2022. This could mean that many bicycles are stolen during the daylight, where bicycles counts are much higher than at night. However, this question requires further investigation, as getting the exact time of a bicycle theft is impossible due to solely a timeframe being provided.

Limitations

  • The data analysed is only for the year 2022, as this is currently the only full year for which all three datasets provide data. In the future, when more data is released, the analysis could be expanded to a new year to reduce the influence of events that could be specific to the year 2022. (f.e. the year before that, COVID lockdowns would have had a significant impact on mobility).
  • The variation in bicycle theft and bicycle counts can obviously involve other factors, namely public holidays, school holidays, events affecting tourism and more.

Conclusion

The results tend to show that it is safer for ones bicycle to be ridden during on warm days and days with little to no rain. In general, these correlations are closely related with the number of other people riding their bike on a given day, which means that being one of the fewer people cycling during bad weather should rather be avoided the reduce the chances of the bike being stolen (and getting wet). However, some of the correlations are not as strong, which could mean that other factors also play a large part. Further research should be done to identify those.

Outlook

  • The datasets could be expanded by using more weather and bicycle counting stations, along with mapping bicycle thefts to their nearest bicycle counting station. This would reduce the assumptions made in the Methods chapter and provide a more realistic view. For example, so some districts of Berlin will likely have higher bicycle theft rates, which is neglected in this analysis.
  • Further research could try to use the data from 2022 to train a Machine Learning model and test it on data that it released in the future.
  • The other parameters from the bicycle theft datasets could be analysed further, for example to see if there is a correlation of the value of stolen bicycles and other factors.
  • The other parameters from weather data could be used as well when looking for correlations with other metrics, for example differentiating between rain and snow.
  • Other datasets, for example for public holidays, could be taken into account.
